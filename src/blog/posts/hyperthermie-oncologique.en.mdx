## What is oncological hyperthermia?

Oncological hyperthermia is a medical technique that involves locally raising the temperature of tumor tissues to between 40°C and 43°C. This controlled increase in heat sensitizes cancer cells, making them more vulnerable to conventional treatments such as chemotherapy and radiotherapy.

Recognized by numerous international scientific studies, hyperthermia has been used for several decades in leading oncology centers in Europe, particularly in Germany and the Netherlands.

## How does hyperthermia work?

The principle is based on the difference in heat response between healthy cells and tumor cells. Cancer cells, due to their disorganized vascularization, dissipate heat less effectively and are therefore more sensitive to temperature elevation.

Hyperthermia acts at multiple levels:

- **Chemotherapy sensitization**: heat increases the permeability of cell membranes, facilitating the penetration of chemotherapeutic agents.
- **Radiotherapy potentiation**: hypoxic cells, often resistant to radiation, become more sensitive under the effect of heat.
- **Immune stimulation**: hyperthermia promotes the release of heat shock proteins (HSP), which stimulate the anti-tumor immune response.

## Main indications

Oncological hyperthermia is indicated in the treatment of numerous types of solid tumors:

- Breast cancer
- Pancreatic cancer
- Colorectal cancer
- Bladder cancer
- Soft tissue sarcomas
- Pelvic tumors (cervix, rectum)

Each indication is subject to an individual medical evaluation to determine the relevance and appropriate protocol.

## What happens during a session

A local hyperthermia session generally lasts between 60 and 90 minutes. The patient is comfortably positioned and the device is placed over the area to be treated. Temperature is continuously monitored to ensure the effectiveness and safety of the treatment.

Sessions are non-invasive and do not require anesthesia. Most patients can resume their normal activities immediately after the session.
