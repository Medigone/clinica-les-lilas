Electromodulated oncological hyperthermia is a complementary therapy that uses heat in a controlled and localised way to sensitise tumour cells and enhance the effectiveness of conventional oncological treatments. Recognised by international scientific studies and used in reference centres in Europe for decades, this non-invasive technique offers a real option for medical support for patients undergoing chemotherapy or radiotherapy treatment.

Clínica Les Lilas is the only centre specialised in electromodulated oncological hyperthermia in Alicante and the entire Costa Blanca. This guide clearly and rigorously explains what the treatment involves, how it works, who it is indicated for and what to expect from the protocol.

## What is electromodulated oncological hyperthermia?

Oncological hyperthermia is a medical technique that consists of locally raising the temperature of tumour tissues to between 39 °C and 43 °C. Unlike conventional hyperthermia, the **electromodulated** modality combines the thermal effect with low-intensity electromagnetic fields, which allows more precise action on the tumour area while minimising impact on surrounding healthy tissue.

This treatment is used exclusively as a complement to conventional oncological treatments — chemotherapy, radiotherapy or immunotherapy — and never as a substitute. Its aim is to increase the effectiveness of these treatments and improve the patient's quality of life during the process.

## How does heat act on tumour cells?

The scientific basis of oncological hyperthermia lies in a fundamental biological difference between healthy and tumour cells: their response to heat.

Tumour cells have chaotic and irregular vascularisation that prevents them from dissipating heat efficiently. When the temperature of the area is raised, cancer cells undergo thermal stress that makes them significantly more vulnerable. Healthy cells, with normal blood supply, regulate their temperature better and withstand treatment without significant damage.

The identified mechanisms of action are:

**Chemotherapy sensitisation.** Heat increases the permeability of tumour cell membranes, facilitating the penetration of chemotherapeutic agents into the cancer cell. This directly enhances the effectiveness of chemotherapy without needing to increase the dose — and therefore without increasing toxicity.

**Radiotherapy potentiation.** Tumour cells in hypoxia — poorly oxygenated areas of the tumour — are usually resistant to radiation. Heat reduces this resistance, allowing radiotherapy to act more completely on tumour tissue.

**Immune system stimulation.** Hyperthermia promotes the production of heat shock proteins (HSP), which act as alarm signals for the immune system, stimulating a more active anti-tumour response.

**Direct pro-apoptotic effect.** At temperatures above 42 °C, heat can directly induce programmed cell death (apoptosis) of the most sensitive tumour cells.

## Scientific evidence: what the studies say

Oncological hyperthermia has decades of clinical research and is recognised by the main European oncology guidelines. Some relevant data:

- A meta-analysis published in *International Journal of Hyperthermia* demonstrated that the combination of hyperthermia + radiotherapy significantly improves tumour response rates compared with radiotherapy alone in solid tumours of the head and neck, breast and cervix.
- Studies from Erasmus Medical Centre in Rotterdam and the University Hospital of Tübingen have confirmed clinical benefits in soft tissue sarcomas, recurrent pelvic tumours and bladder cancers.
- The European Society for Hyperthermic Oncology (ESHO) and the American Society for Hyperthermic Oncology (ASHO) recognise hyperthermia as a complementary therapeutic modality with solid evidence in specific indications.

## Main indications

Electromodulated oncological hyperthermia is indicated as a complementary treatment in the context of numerous types of solid tumours. The most common indications are:

- Breast cancer (locally advanced or recurrent)
- Colorectal cancer
- Pancreatic cancer
- Bladder cancer
- Soft tissue sarcomas
- Pelvic tumours (cervix, rectum, prostate)
- Non-small cell lung cancer

Each case is evaluated individually. The suitability of treatment depends on factors such as tumour type, location, the patient's general condition and the conventional treatment in progress. The therapeutic decision is always made jointly with the oncology team.

## The protocol at Clínica Les Lilas: from first consultation to last session

**First medical consultation.** Before starting any treatment, the patient undergoes a thorough evaluation: oncological history, treatments in progress, blood tests, available imaging. Contact is established with the patient's referring oncologist.

**Personalised protocol design.** Based on the evaluation, the number of sessions is defined (usually between 10 and 12), their frequency (generally 1–2 per week) and their coordination with chemotherapy or radiotherapy cycles.

**The sessions.** Each session lasts between 60 and 90 minutes. The patient remains in a comfortable position while the team positions the applicator on the area to be treated. Temperature is monitored continuously. Sessions are non-invasive, do not require anaesthesia and allow normal activity to be resumed immediately.

**Medical follow-up.** Throughout the protocol, periodic reviews are carried out to adjust treatment according to the patient's response and tolerance.

Hyperthermia does not irradiate, does not destroy healthy tissue and does not generate the side effects typical of radiotherapy. It is precisely its safety profile that makes it a valid support option for patients already undergoing intensive treatments.

## Is it painful? Safety and side effects

Electromodulated oncological hyperthermia is a treatment well tolerated by the vast majority of patients. During the session, the usual sensation is one of deep, progressive warmth in the treated area, similar to the application of therapeutic heat.

Side effects are uncommon and generally mild:

- Sensation of heat or mild superficial skin redness, transient
- Slight fatigue after the session in some patients

No serious side effects have been described with the electromodulated protocol when applied within the established therapeutic ranges and under medical supervision.
